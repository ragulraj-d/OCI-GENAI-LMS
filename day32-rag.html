The Business Problem
Why LTIMindtree needs RAG — Arjun's daily struggle

Meet Arjun. He is an IT Support Engineer at LTIMindtree's Hyderabad office. His job sounds simple — answer employee questions about company policies. But the reality is a nightmare.

LTIMindtree has 1,500+ pages of policy documents spread across four heavy PDFs:

📄 HR Policy PDF — 847 pages  |  📄 IT Security PDF — 312 pages  |  📄 Finance PDF — 156 pages  |  📄 Leave Policy PDF — 203 pages

Every single day, 40 to 50 employees ping Arjun with questions like: "How many leaves do I get?", "What is the WFH policy?", "How do I get my laptop replaced?"

For every question, Arjun opens the right PDF, presses Ctrl+F, reads through 15 matching sections, finds the correct paragraph, and types a reply email. That is 20–30 minutes per query. With 40 queries a day, Arjun needs 13–25 hours of work — but has only 8 hours. He is always behind. Employees wait hours. Sometimes he gives the wrong answer from memory. That's a compliance risk.

The CTO's directive: "Build an AI assistant that reads our policy documents and answers employee questions instantly. It must answer ONLY from our official documents — not from ChatGPT's training data, not from the internet. From our PDFs only."

That requirement has a name: RAG — Retrieval Augmented Generation.

🚨

Hallucination Risk: When an LLM doesn't know the answer, it doesn't say "I don't know." It confidently generates a plausible-sounding answer that may be completely wrong. In a corporate policy context, a wrong answer can lead to compliance violations, employee disputes, and legal risk.

Two Fundamental Problems with Plain LLMs for Company Data

Problem 1 — No Company Knowledge

LLMs like Cohere or GPT are trained on public internet data. They have never seen LTIMindtree's internal policy documents. They cannot tell you that LTIMindtree gives exactly 18 days of annual leave, because that specific fact exists only in LTIMindtree's private PDF.

Problem 2 — Knowledge Cutoff

LLMs have a training cutoff date. If LTIMindtree updated their leave policy last month, the LLM doesn't know about it. A new policy was uploaded? LLM still answers with the old information. RAG always reads the latest uploaded document.

Analogy — The New Doctor

You go to a new hospital. The doctor is extremely intelligent — they have studied medicine for 20 years and know treatments for thousands of diseases. But they have never seen your personal medical file before.

You ask: "What medicines am I allergic to?"

The doctor guesses based on general medical knowledge. That guess could be dangerous. The right approach: the doctor reads YOUR file first, then answers based on YOUR specific medical history.

RAG = giving the LLM YOUR company documents before it answers. The LLM reads, then answers. It doesn't guess from memory.

Definition

What is RAG?

For Common Man

You hired a very smart assistant who has read millions of books. But they haven't read your company documents. RAG = before answering any question, you first give them the relevant pages from your documents. They read those pages, then answer from what they just read — not from their general memory.

For Business Person

AI that answers from YOUR knowledge base. Not from internet. Not from general training. From your specific policies, manuals, and documents. Every answer is traceable to a source paragraph. Accurate, auditable, company-specific answers at scale.

For Technical Person

RAG = Retrieval Augmented Generation. At query time: (1) convert question to vector, (2) search vector store for similar document chunks, (3) inject retrieved chunks into LLM prompt, (4) LLM generates answer constrained to retrieved content. Formula: Answer = LLM(Question + Retrieved Chunks).

Formal Definition

RAG is a natural language processing technique that combines information retrieval with large language model generation. It retrieves relevant context from an external knowledge base at inference time and uses it to ground the LLM's response, reducing hallucination and enabling domain-specific accuracy.

💡

The RAG Formula: Without RAG: Answer = LLM(Question only) — LLM uses training memory → may hallucinate.   With RAG: Answer = LLM(Question + Retrieved Document Chunks) — LLM reads real content → accurate answer.

Most Important Concept

RAG Has TWO Completely Separate Pipelines

⚠️

Students always confuse these two pipelines. Learn this before anything else. If you understand only one thing from Day 37, let it be this: RAG has an Ingestion Pipeline (runs once) and a Query Pipeline (runs every time someone asks a question). They are completely separate.

Pipeline 1 — Ingestion

Runs ONCE (or when documents change). Purpose: prepare documents for searching. Input: PDF files. Output: vectors stored in a vector database. This is the setup phase.

Pipeline 2 — Query

Runs EVERY TIME an employee asks a question. Purpose: find relevant content and generate an answer. Input: employee question. Output: accurate answer from document content.

Analogy — Building a Library vs Visiting a Library

Building the library (Ingestion): You collect all books, organize them, label them, and place them on shelves with a catalog system. This takes time but happens only once.

Visiting the library (Query): Every time a student wants to find information, they use the catalog to find the right shelf, pick the right book, read the relevant pages, and answer their question. This is fast because the library is already organized.

RAG works exactly this way. Ingestion = building the library. Query = visiting the library. You build once, search many times.

Core Concepts

The Four Core Components of RAG

① Document Store

Where your original documents live. Not a vector store — just the source files. For LTIMindtree: the 4 policy PDFs. In production: OCI Object Storage bucket. In labs: text string in Python.

📚 Analogy: The physical library shelves where books are stored. You need a catalog to find what's inside.

② Embedding Model

Converts text into arrays of numbers (vectors). Similar meaning → similar numbers. Different meaning → different numbers. Enables computers to compare meaning mathematically.

🔢 Input: "laptop replacement" → Output: [0.23, 0.87, 0.12, 0.45...]

③ Vector Store (Retriever)

Stores two things per chunk: the vector (for searching) and the original text (returned after search). Enables meaning-based similarity search — much better than keyword search.

🗄️ In Labs: ChromaDB (in-memory). In Production: OCI OpenSearch.

④ LLM Generator

The final step. Reads question + retrieved chunks. Writes the answer. The LLM does NOT search, does NOT retrieve. It ONLY reads what the retriever found and writes a coherent answer.

🤖 For LTIMindtree: OCI Cohere model (cohere.command-a-03-2025)

🔑

Critical Rule — What Vector Store Stores: Vector store stores TWO things per chunk: (1) the vector (numbers) for searching, and (2) the original text for returning to the LLM. Without original text, the LLM would receive numbers like [0.23, 0.87...] which it cannot read. With original text: it receives "Employees are entitled to 18 days..." which it can use to answer.

Deep Dive

Understanding Embeddings

Embeddings are the heart of RAG. Without embeddings, we cannot do meaning-based search. Let's understand them completely.

What is an Embedding?

An embedding is the conversion of text into a list of numbers (a vector). The key property: similar meaning = similar numbers. The computer can then compare meanings mathematically.

What embeddings look like
# Text → Embedding (array of numbers)

"laptop replacement"  → [0.23, 0.87, 0.12, 0.45, ...]  # 384 numbers
"computer upgrade"    → [0.21, 0.89, 0.11, 0.43, ...]  # similar!
"leave application"  → [0.78, 0.12, 0.91, 0.33, ...]  # very different!

# "laptop replacement" and "computer upgrade" are similar in meaning
# → their vectors are close together in mathematical space
# "leave application" has very different meaning
# → its vector is far away from the first two
Analogy — GPS Coordinates

Think of Chennai (13.08°N, 80.27°E), Bangalore (12.97°N, 77.59°E), and Tokyo (35.68°N, 139.69°E).

Chennai and Bangalore are geographically close → their coordinates are similar. Tokyo is far away → very different coordinates.

Text embeddings work the same way. "laptop policy" and "computer rules" have similar meaning → close vectors. "laptop policy" and "leave policy" have different meanings → far vectors. The computer uses these distances to find relevant content.

Vector Store & Similarity Search

How Similarity Search Actually Works

This is NOT keyword search. This is meaning-based search. Here is the step-by-step flow:

1
Employee asks a question
"How do I get a new computer?" — note they said "computer", not "laptop"
2
Question converted to a vector
Embedding model converts the question: [0.22, 0.85, 0.13...] (384 numbers)
3
Vector compared against ALL stored chunk vectors
ChromaDB calculates the distance between the question vector and every stored chunk vector using Cosine Similarity
4
Closest vectors selected (Top K)
Chunk 5 has vector [0.23, 0.87, 0.12...] — very close to question vector. Even though the chunk says "laptop" and question says "computer", they are semantically similar. Chunk 5 is retrieved.
5
Original text returned
"Laptop replacement after 3 years of use..." — the original human-readable text is returned to the LLM, not the vector numbers

Keyword Search vs Semantic Search

❌ Keyword Search (Old Way)

Search "laptop" → finds "laptop" only. Search "computer" → finds different results. Same topic, different word → misses relevant content. Used by Ctrl+F in PDFs.

✅ Semantic / Vector Search

Search "laptop" → finds "computer", "device", "hardware" too. Understands meaning. Returns ALL relevant content even if exact words differ. RAG uses this.

Cosine Similarity — How Similarity is Measured

📐

Cosine Similarity measures the angle between two vectors. Score of 1.0 = identical meaning (same direction). Score of 0.9 = very similar. Score of 0.5 = somewhat related. Score of 0.1 = unrelated (perpendicular). ChromaDB uses this to rank chunks by relevance.

Document Processing

Chunking Strategies

Why Do We Need Chunking?

Problem 1 — Context Window Limit

LLMs can only read a limited amount of text per call. Cohere: ~128K tokens max. LTIMindtree's HR Policy PDF: 847 pages ≈ 400,000+ tokens. Exceeds the limit. Cannot send the whole PDF.

Problem 2 — Retrieval Precision

Sending 100 pages for one question floods the LLM. It gets confused with too much information and gives generic, imprecise answers. Focused 3–5 chunk retrieval gives much better answers.

Analogy — Textbook Index

You have an 847-page textbook. Someone asks about the laptop replacement policy. Do you read all 847 pages? No. You check the index: "Laptop Policy → page 312". You open page 312, read that section, answer the question.

Chunking = creating that indexed section system. Each chunk = one indexable section. Retriever = the index search. You retrieve only the 3 most relevant sections instead of all 847 pages.

Strategy 1 — Fixed Size Chunking

Split document every N characters. Ignores sentence boundaries, paragraph breaks, and meaning.

❌

Problem: "leave must be appro" / "ved by manager" — the word "approved" is split across two chunks. Neither chunk has a complete sentence. Meaning is destroyed. LLM receives broken text.

Use when: Quick prototypes only. Never in production.

Strategy 2 — Recursive Text Splitter ✅ (Most Common)

Tries natural boundaries in priority order: paragraph (\n\n) → line break (\n) → sentence (". ") → word (" ") → character. Preserves complete sentences and natural reading flow.

✅

Result: "Employees are entitled to 18 days of paid annual leave per calendar year." — Complete sentence. Full meaning preserved. LangChain class: RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter.

Use when: Most business documents, policy PDFs, manuals. This is what we use in our labs.

Strategy 3 — Semantic Chunking

Splits when the TOPIC changes, detected using embeddings. If two consecutive sentences have very different vectors, start a new chunk.

"Employees get 18 days leave." ← leave topic → same chunk "Leave must be approved by manager." ← leave topic → same chunk "Laptop must be returned on exit." ← IT topic → NEW CHUNK HERE! "Data must be wiped before return." ← IT topic → same chunk

Use when: Complex documents, legal content, highly technical manuals. Best quality but slower and more expensive.

Chunk Size — No Universal Correct Value

⚠️

There is no single "correct" chunk size. The right value depends on your document structure, retrieval quality, and use case. Recommendation for policy documents: Start with chunk_size=300, chunk_overlap=50. Test retrieval quality. Adjust based on results. Too small = broken sentences. Too large = noisy retrieval.

ParameterSmall ValueLarge ValueRecommended
chunk_sizePrecise retrieval, risk of broken contextMore context, more noise300–500 chars for policy docs
chunk_overlapNo boundary context preservationRedundant data, larger index10–20% of chunk_size

Chunk Overlap — Preventing Information Loss at Boundaries

Without Overlap

Chunk 1 ends: "...laptop after 3 years. Requests"
Chunk 2 starts: "must be submitted to IT helpdesk..."
The sentence "Requests must be submitted" is split. Neither chunk has complete context.

With Overlap (50 chars)

Chunk 1 ends: "...after 3 years. Requests must"
Chunk 2 starts: "Requests must be submitted to IT..."
50 characters are shared. The key phrase appears in both chunks. Context preserved.

Chunk Drift — A Silent Problem

🚨

Chunk Drift = one chunk contains content from two different topics. Happens at section boundaries. Example: "...18 days leave. [DRIFT] Laptop must be returned..." → Employee asks "How many leave days?" → LLM gets confused chunk mixing leave and laptop topics → gives incorrect answer. Fix: use semantic chunking or section headers to force chunk boundaries.

Production Search

ANN — Approximate Nearest Neighbor Search

Why Not Exact Search in Production?

In our labs we have 9 chunks. ChromaDB compares the question vector against all 9. Fast. No problem. But LTIMindtree in production might have 1 million chunks across all their documents.

Exact Search — 1M vectors

Compare question vector against ALL 1,000,000 stored vectors one by one. Always finds the true nearest neighbor. But: too slow for production. Like reading every page of 1,500 policy documents to find one answer.

ANN Search — 1M vectors

Smart shortcut. Finds approximate nearest neighbors. 95–99% accurate. 100× faster than exact search. Good enough for RAG. Production always uses ANN.

Analogy — Lost Keys in a Hotel

Exact search: Lost your keys in a 100-room hotel. You search every single room systematically. Eventually find them. Perfect result. But took 2 hours.

ANN search: You know you were near the lobby area. You search lobby, reception, and 5 nearby rooms. Find keys in 5 minutes. Not exhaustive — but fast and good enough.

RAG needs speed. ANN is the right choice. Finding the "good enough" nearest neighbor in milliseconds is far more valuable than finding the "perfect" one in seconds.

Three ANN Index Types

Index TypeHow It WorksSpeedAccuracyBest For
Flat IndexNo indexing. Compare question with every vector exactly.SlowestPerfect (100%)Small datasets < 10K chunks
IVF (Inverted File)Clusters vectors into groups. Search nearest cluster only.Fast95–98%Medium: 100K–10M vectors
HNSWGraph structure. Similar vectors connected. Navigate graph to find nearest.Fastest98–99%Production default — any scale
ℹ️

HNSW = Hierarchical Navigable Small World. The default algorithm in ChromaDB, FAISS, and Milvus. Builds a multi-layer graph where similar vectors are connected as neighbors. Search starts at top layer (few nodes, long connections) and progressively navigates to bottom layer (all nodes, precise matches). For LTIMindtree's labs with 9 chunks: Flat index. For production with 1M+ chunks: HNSW.

Retrieval Strategies

Dense, Sparse, and Hybrid Retrieval

Dense Retrieval (Vector)

Uses embedding vectors. Finds chunks by meaning. "laptop" → also finds "computer", "device". Good for: semantic understanding, synonyms, related concepts. Our labs use this.

Weakness: may miss exact rare terms like "Section 3.2.1"

Sparse Retrieval (BM25)

Keyword-based using term frequency. Finds exact words. "LTIMindtree" → finds only LTIMindtree docs. Good for: exact terms, policy section numbers, specific codes.

Weakness: "laptop" doesn't find "computer"

Hybrid Retrieval ✅ Best

Combines BM25 + vector search. Results merged using RRF (Reciprocal Rank Fusion). Gets benefits of both. Maximum recall. Production recommendation for enterprise RAG.

Best accuracy for most use cases

Metadata Filtering

After vector search, you can also filter by metadata. This dramatically improves precision.

✅

Example: Employee from HR department asks about leave. You can filter: WHERE source="leave_policy.pdf" AND department="HR". This skips all IT and Finance policy chunks entirely, even if they have somewhat similar vectors. More precise. Smaller search space. Faster response.

LLM Integration

Prompt Anatomy — What Goes Into the LLM?

A complete RAG prompt has three distinct parts. Understanding this is essential for production RAG.

Part 1 — System Prompt

Who the assistant is. What rules it must follow. Constraints on behavior. This is what prevents hallucination by restricting the LLM to only what is in the retrieved text.

Part 2 — Retrieved Chunks

The actual policy text we retrieved from ChromaDB. This is the "open book" the LLM reads from. The LLM must confine its answer to this content only.

Part 3 — User Question

What the employee actually asked. Placed after the retrieved context so the LLM has the full context before seeing the specific question to answer.

Complete RAG Prompt Structure
# PART 1 — SYSTEM PROMPT (prevents hallucination)
system_prompt = """You are LTIMindtree's HR Policy Assistant.
Answer using ONLY the policy text provided below.
If the answer is not in the policy, say:
  'This is not covered in the current policy document.'
Do NOT make up any information.
Be professional and concise."""

# PART 2 — RETRIEVED CHUNKS (the open book)
retrieved_chunks = """
[Chunk 1]: Employees are entitled to 18 days of paid annual leave...
[Chunk 2]: Leave must be approved by the reporting manager...
[Chunk 3]: Unused annual leave cannot be carried forward...
"""

# PART 3 — USER QUESTION
question = "How many leave days do I get and can I carry them forward?"

# COMBINED PROMPT SENT TO OCI COHERE
full_prompt = f"""{system_prompt}

POLICY TEXT:
{retrieved_chunks}

EMPLOYEE QUESTION: {question}

ANSWER:"""
⚠️

Why System Prompt Prevents Hallucination: Without "Answer ONLY from policy text provided", the LLM might mix retrieved content with its general training knowledge. It might recall vague memories from the internet and blend them with the actual policy. The system prompt creates a hard constraint: use ONLY what I gave you. If it's not in the retrieved chunks, say so.

RAG Variants

Standard RAG vs Agentic RAG

Standard RAG — Fixed Pipeline

Every question goes through the same steps every time. No decision making. No adaptation. Fast, predictable, cost-effective.

Question → Embed → Search → Top 3 → LLM → Answer

Best for: Factual policy Q&A. LTIMindtree's use case. 95% of enterprise RAG deployments.

Agentic RAG — Agent Decides

Agent decides WHEN to search, HOW MANY times, and WHETHER to search again. Uses reasoning loops. Can use other tools. Self-corrects.

Best for: Multi-hop questions like "Compare LTIMindtree's leave policy with competitors." Requires external search for missing context.

LAB 1 Ingest Documents into ChromaDB
Why this lab exists: LLMs cannot directly search PDFs. We must first prepare documents for searching. This lab takes policy text, splits it into chunks, and stores them in ChromaDB. ChromaDB automatically handles embedding — it converts each chunk to a vector using its built-in default model. You don't need to call an embedding model separately.
lab1_ingest.py
from langchain.text_splitter import RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter
import chromadb

# LTIMindtree policy text
# In production: read from actual PDF files using pypdf
# In labs: text string to focus on RAG concepts
policy_text = """
SECTION 1: ANNUAL LEAVE POLICY
Employees are entitled to 18 days of paid annual leave per calendar year.
The leave year runs from January 1st to December 31st.
All leave requests must be submitted at least 5 working days in advance.
Leave must be approved by the reporting manager before being taken.
Unused annual leave cannot be carried forward to the next year.
Leave encashment is allowed for a maximum of 5 days per year.

SECTION 2: SICK LEAVE POLICY
Employees are entitled to 12 days of sick leave per year.
Sick leave can be taken without prior approval in case of emergency.
For sick leave exceeding 3 consecutive days, a medical certificate is mandatory.
Sick leave cannot be encashed or carried forward.

SECTION 3: LAPTOP REPLACEMENT POLICY
Employees are eligible for laptop replacement after 3 years of use.
Replacement requests must be submitted to IT helpdesk via the IT portal.
Approved replacements will be processed within 15 working days.
Employees must return the old laptop within 5 days of receiving the new one.

SECTION 4: WORK FROM HOME POLICY
Employees can work from home up to 2 days per week.
WFH must be approved by the reporting manager on a weekly basis.
WFH is not allowed on Mondays and Fridays without special approval.
"""

# STEP 1: CHUNKING
# RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter tries to split on natural boundaries
# chunk_size: max characters per chunk (start small, test, adjust)
# chunk_overlap: characters shared between consecutive chunks
#                prevents information loss at boundaries
splitter = RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter(
    chunk_size=300,
    chunk_overlap=50
)
chunks = splitter.split_text(policy_text)

print(f"Total chunks created: {len(chunks)}")
for i, chunk in enumerate(chunks):
    print(f"\nCHUNK {i+1} ({len(chunk)} chars):")
    print(chunk)

# STEP 2: STORE IN CHROMADB
# ChromaDB automatically embeds each document using its built-in model
# Each chunk stored as: vector (for search) + original text (for retrieval)
# No need to call embedding model explicitly — ChromaDB handles it
client = chromadb.Client()
collection = client.create_collection("ltimindtree_policies")

collection.add(
    documents=chunks,
    ids=[f"chunk_{i}" for i in range(len(chunks))]
)

print(f"\nStored {len(chunks)} chunks in ChromaDB.")
print("Each chunk: vector (for search) + text (for answer)")
print("Ready for LAB 2.")

RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter
LangChain class that splits text on natural boundaries. Tries paragraph breaks first, then line breaks, then sentence ends, then word spaces. Preserves complete sentences unlike fixed-size splitting.
chunk_size=300
Maximum characters per chunk. Not too small (broken context) and not too large (noisy retrieval). 300 works well for policy documents.
chunk_overlap=50
50 characters are shared between consecutive chunks. Prevents key information from being split across chunk boundaries.
chromadb.Client()
Creates an in-memory ChromaDB instance. Resets when Python script stops. For production, use persist_directory to save to disk.
create_collection()
Creates a named collection (like a table in a database) to store your chunks.

Stores each chunk. ChromaDB automatically embeds each chunk using its default model. Both the vector and original text are stored together.

# SIMILARITY SEARCH
# ChromaDB:
#   1. Converts question to vector using same embedding model
#   2. Compares with ALL stored chunk vectors
#   3. Returns top_k most similar chunks (original text, not vectors)
# This is NOT keyword search — it's meaning-based similarity search!
def retrieve(question, top_k=3):
    print(f"QUESTION: {question}")
    print()

    results = collection.query(
        query_texts=[question],
        n_results=top_k
    )

    print(f"TOP {top_k} RETRIEVED CHUNKS:")
    for i, doc in enumerate(results["documents"][0]):
        print(f"  [{i+1}] {doc[:80]}...")
    print()
    return results["documents"][0]

Interview Preparation

Interview Questions — Beginner to Advanced

Beginner Level

What is RAG and why is it needed?
RAG stands for Retrieval Augmented Generation. It is needed because LLMs are trained on general internet data and don't know company-specific information. When an LLM doesn't know an answer, it hallucinates — generates a plausible-sounding but wrong answer. RAG solves this by retrieving relevant text from your own documents before answering, so the LLM reads your actual content rather than guessing.
What are the two pipelines in RAG?
RAG has two completely separate pipelines: (1) Ingestion Pipeline — runs once when documents are uploaded. Takes PDFs, extracts text, splits into chunks, converts to vectors, stores in a vector database. (2) Query Pipeline — runs every time a user asks a question. Converts the question to a vector, searches the vector database for similar chunks, retrieves top K chunks, builds a prompt combining question and chunks, sends to the LLM, which generates an answer.
What is an embedding?
An embedding is the conversion of text into an array of numbers called a vector. The key property is that similar meaning = similar numbers. "laptop replacement" and "computer upgrade" have similar meaning, so their embedding vectors are mathematically close. This allows computers to compare text meaning numerically, enabling semantic search.

Intermediate Level

What does the vector store actually store?
The vector store stores two things per chunk: (1) the vector (array of numbers) used for similarity searching, and (2) the original text returned to the LLM after retrieval. Without the original text, the LLM would receive meaningless numbers. With it, the LLM receives the actual policy text it needs to answer the question.
What is Cosine Similarity and how is it used in RAG?
Cosine Similarity measures the angle between two vectors. Score of 1.0 means identical meaning (same direction), 0.9 means very similar, 0.1 means unrelated. In RAG, when a question is converted to a vector, ChromaDB calculates cosine similarity between the question vector and all stored chunk vectors. The chunks with the highest similarity scores are retrieved as the most relevant content.
What is chunk overlap and why is it needed?
Chunk overlap is when a specified number of characters (e.g., 50) are shared between consecutive chunks. Without overlap, a key sentence that falls at the boundary between two chunks would be split — part in chunk N and part in chunk N+1. Neither chunk has complete context. With overlap, that sentence appears fully in both chunks, ensuring no information is lost at boundaries. Rule of thumb: overlap = 10-20% of chunk size.
What is the difference between keyword search and vector search?
Keyword search (like Ctrl+F or BM25) finds documents that contain the exact words. Searching for "laptop" will not find "computer" even if they refer to the same thing. Vector search is meaning-based — "laptop" would also find "computer", "device", "machine" because they have similar embedding vectors. Vector search understands semantics, not just lexical matches.

Advanced Level

What is ANN search and why is it used instead of exact search in production?
ANN = Approximate Nearest Neighbor. Exact search compares a query vector against every single stored vector — perfect accuracy but O(n) complexity that becomes too slow at scale (e.g., 1 million chunks). ANN uses indexing structures (Flat, IVF, HNSW) to find approximate nearest neighbors much faster — 100× faster than exact search with 95-99% accuracy. HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) is the production default in ChromaDB and FAISS.
What is the difference between Standard RAG and Agentic RAG?
Standard RAG has a fixed pipeline: every question goes through embed → search → retrieve → generate. No decision-making. Best for factual Q&A. Agentic RAG uses an AI agent that decides when to search, how many times to search, whether to refine the query, and can use other tools (web search, APIs). Best for multi-hop questions needing data from multiple sources. Standard RAG is faster and cheaper; Agentic RAG is more flexible and powerful.
When would you use hybrid search vs pure vector search?
Hybrid search (BM25 + vector) is preferred when: (1) users may use exact technical terms like policy section numbers, product codes, or specific identifiers that vector search might miss; (2) you need maximum recall across diverse query types; (3) your documents have both natural language sections and structured data. Pure vector search is sufficient when queries are consistently natural language and exact keyword matching is not critical, such as general policy Q&A.
Why use temperature=0.1 for RAG but higher values for creative tasks?
Temperature controls randomness/creativity in LLM output. Temperature=0.1 means very deterministic — the model nearly always picks the highest probability next token. For RAG, we want: the same accurate answer every time for the same question. No creativity, no paraphrasing variation. For creative writing tasks, temperature=0.7-0.9 produces varied, creative outputs. Policy Q&A requires consistency and accuracy, not creativity, hence temperature=0.1.
